Abstract

This study is part of my work as a student assistant in the context of the Max Planck Research Group [Governance of the Universal Church after the Council of Trent](https://www.lhlt.mpg.de/research-group/governance-of-the-universal-church-after-the-council-of-trent) active at the Max Planck Institute for Legal History and Legal Theory between 2014 and 2021.
The task of developing an ontology is a crucial step from the data model for the data acquired by the research group towards an RDF export option.
Discussions about the wider data model and about corner cases as well as individual records for which the ontology was developed are among the research group's activities and products. Vice versa, the ontology developed here is an important foundation for refinements and additions that will finally make the research group's main export option and the main lever with which to insert its data into the global linked data landscape.

Introduction back to ToC

    The characterizing aspect of the data set about ecclesiastical institutions is a large number of events/changes which can be considered in terms of the distinction between three aspects: biography, legality, and locality.

    Since the need for data-based historical research is steadily increasing, it is essential to build a database based on semantic technology and ontology. Therefore, building a domain ontology is the first step towards publishing historical information in a structured and linked way.

Adopting an event-based approach, we semantically modeled the administrative relationships of the ecclesiastical institutions of the Catholic Church and their changes. The prototype work is an extension to the CIDOC-CRM, an existing ontology in the field of cultural heritage. Its key notions were defined at the top level of the ontology (Change as Event, Ecclesiastical Institution as an agent as well as Time, Place and Source) and were customized by the concept of domain-specific knowledge.

Construction Methods back to ToC

The construction basically consisted of three stages: 1. Generation of the abstract draft model based on the data set and reviewed by the research team.
2. Attachment and alignment of the graph to the CIDOC-CRM, which required considerable modification of the class hierarchy and the relationships.
3. Visualization of the ontology using the Protégé plug-in OntoGraf and VOWL, a tool for the interactive visualization of ontologies.

SCC Ontology: Description back to ToC

Using an event-based approach makes it possible to model hierarchy and status of each entity and their changes, e.g. administrative status of an ecclesiastical institution. There are three main classes: * *change* is the central class of the ontology, which is used to model the events of the administrative structure, which is subdivided into the subclasses ’governing change’, ’status change’, and ’territorial change’ depending on the characteristics of the administrative event. * *religious_administrative_unit* comprises various ecclesiastical institutions and the Roman Curia is involved as an agent of the activities related. * *crm:E53_Place* contains the geographical information within the prototype ontology such as names, coordinates, and types of places.

Conclusion & Future Works back to ToC

This work has shown that CIDOC-CRM is a suitable framework for creating ontologies to represent administrative events based on canon law. However, future work should consider taking the following steps to further refine and extend the prototype:
1. Link similar terms from different vocabularies or thesauri in external ontologies, such as LODE, DBPedia, Europeana Data Model, and Wikidata.
2. Mapping all entries of the Dataset in the form of triples and assigning URI to them
3. On the technical side: building a knowledge graph database and making the RDF triples mapped in this work available for distribution via the programming interface.
